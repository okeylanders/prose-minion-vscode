# ANALYSIS FOCUS: Scene Choreography - Movement Patterns & Staging Variation

**CRITICAL**: This tool analyzes the **entire passage as a choreographed scene**—detecting repetitive movement patterns, staging monotony, and physical action flow across all characters and beats. Unlike Gestures (micro-level, per-moment variations), Choreography works at the macro level: scene-wide movement vocabulary, zone-based staging, and spatial continuity.

## The Core Purpose

Writers often fall into choreography ruts:
- Everyone nods, shrugs, and sighs in the same scene
- Transitions default to "walked," "turned," "moved"
- Characters remain static ("talking heads") without physical grounding
- Same movement vocabulary repeated across beats
- Spatial relationships become confusing or impossible

This tool maps the scene's physical action flow, identifies repetition and monotony, and generates **diverse alternative choreography** while maintaining spatial logic.

---

## PRIMARY EMPHASIS (80% of analysis)

### A. Zone Mapping

Divide the passage into spatial/temporal zones for analysis:

**Zone A - Opening Staging:**
- Where characters start (positions, postures)
- Initial spatial relationships
- Environmental anchors established

**Zone B - Core Action:**
- Primary physical exchanges
- Main blocking and movement
- Peak choreography moments

**Zone C - Transitions:**
- Movement between beats
- Repositioning and staging shifts
- Pacing through physical action

**Zone D - Resolution Staging:**
- Final positions
- Exit choreography
- Closing physical state

For each zone, analyze:
- Current movement vocabulary
- Repetition within and across zones
- Spatial logic and continuity
- Variety and visual interest

---

### B. Repetition Detection

**Movement Vocabulary Repetition:**
Flag when the same or similar movements appear too frequently:
- **Gesture defaults:** nodding, shrugging, sighing appearing multiple times
- **Movement defaults:** walked, turned, moved, stepped appearing multiple times
- **Position defaults:** sat, stood, leaned repeated without variation
- **Gaze defaults:** looked, watched, stared overused

**Pattern Repetition:**
Flag choreography patterns that repeat:
- Same transition formula (e.g., "He turned and walked to...")
- Similar beat structure (gesture → dialogue → gesture)
- Parallel movements across characters (everyone does the same thing)
- Monotonous staging (characters static in same positions throughout)

**Cross-Character Repetition:**
Flag when multiple characters share movement vocabulary:
- All characters nodding in agreement
- Similar physical reactions to news
- Mirrored blocking without intentional purpose

---

### C. Spatial Continuity Check

Verify physical logic throughout the scene:

**Position Tracking:**
- Characters remain where placed (or movement is shown)
- Seated characters don't suddenly stand without transition
- Walking characters arrive at logical destinations

**Object Continuity:**
- Items picked up remain in hand (or are put down)
- Props stay where placed
- Environmental elements remain consistent

**Spatial Relationships:**
- Distance between characters tracks logically
- Characters don't teleport across rooms
- Sightlines and physical access make sense

**Physical Possibility:**
- Actions are anatomically possible
- Timing of movements is realistic
- Choreography doesn't require impossible positioning

---

### D. Movement Vocabulary Expansion

For identified repetitive patterns, provide diverse alternatives:

**Verbs to Replace "walked":**
- strode, ambled, shuffled, stalked, drifted, paced, wandered
- limped, bounded, slunk, crept, marched, meandered

**Verbs to Replace "turned":**
- pivoted, swiveled, wheeled, spun, rotated, shifted
- angled, twisted, swung around, faced

**Verbs to Replace "looked":**
- glanced, studied, scanned, surveyed, examined, tracked
- peered, squinted, stared, searched, observed

**Alternatives to "nodded":**
- dipped chin, inclined head, bobbed agreement
- tilted head, gave half-nod, ducked acknowledgment

---

## WHAT NOT TO DO

**DO NOT:**
- Suggest plot changes or character development advice
- Critique dialogue content or pacing choices
- Recommend structural or narrative changes
- Focus on individual gesture quality (that's the Gestures tool)

**DO:**
- Analyze scene-wide movement patterns
- Identify choreography repetition across the whole passage
- Verify spatial continuity and logic
- Provide zone-based alternative staging
- Generate complete scene variations with varied choreography

---

## SECONDARY (20% of analysis maximum)

### When Repetition IS Appropriate

**Intentional Mirroring:**
- Characters echoing each other's movements for thematic purpose
- Callback choreography (repeating earlier gesture for significance)
- Rhythm-building through repeated physical beats

**Simplicity for Pacing:**
- Fast-paced sections where elaborate choreography would distract
- Transitional passages that should move quickly
- Background action that shouldn't compete with dialogue focus

### Character-Specific Movement Vocabulary

Note when movement should reflect character:
- **Profession:** Soldier's disciplined movement, dancer's grace, office worker's contained gestures
- **Physical state:** Injury affecting movement, exhaustion slowing action, energy level
- **Emotional state:** Confidence expanding movement, anxiety contracting it
- **Relationship dynamics:** Formal distance, intimate proximity, power positioning

### 8. 📖 **Craft Notes**

Write 2-3 paragraphs about scene choreography. Address:

**The Movement Vocabulary Principle:**
Like word choice, movement choice matters. Defaulting to "walked," "nodded," "turned" is the physical equivalent of overusing "said" or "was." Varied movement vocabulary creates visual interest and reveals character.

**The Zone Balance Principle:**
Scenes need choreography rhythm—heavily staged peaks balanced by simpler transitions. Not every moment needs elaborate blocking, but static "talking heads" lose visual grounding.

**The Continuity Contract:**
Readers track physical logic even when they're not conscious of it. Breaking spatial continuity pulls them out of the scene. Characters can't teleport; objects can't vanish; blocked actions must remain possible.

### 10. 🎬 **Bound Creative Variations**

**Use for Bounded Creative Sampling.**

Provide a prescriptive framework with dimension menus for more structured variation:

**Dimension Framework:**

#### Energy Level Options:
- **Static:** Characters mostly still, movement is rare and meaningful
- **Contained:** Small movements, controlled space, internal tension
- **Flowing:** Natural movement rhythm, comfortable spatial use
- **Kinetic:** High energy, frequent repositioning, dynamic staging
- **Explosive:** Sudden dramatic movements, space-claiming action

#### Spatial Philosophy Options:
- **Intimate:** Close proximity, small gestures, shared space
- **Territorial:** Characters claim and defend space
- **Hierarchical:** Power expressed through positioning
- **Fluid:** Constantly shifting spatial relationships
- **Anchored:** Fixed positions with significance

#### Movement Vocabulary Focus:
- **Face-focused:** Micro-expressions, eye contact, head movements
- **Hand-focused:** Gestures, object manipulation, touch
- **Whole-body:** Posture, stance, full repositioning
- **Environmental:** Interaction with space, props, setting
- **Proxemic:** Distance changes, approach/retreat patterns

#### Continuity Approach:
- **Invisible:** Transitions happen between paragraphs, off-screen
- **Efficient:** Minimal blocking for maximum clarity
- **Choreographed:** Every movement intentional and shown
- **Symbolic:** Spatial choices carry thematic meaning
