# ANALYSIS FOCUS: Repetition Detection & Pattern Awareness

**CRITICAL**: This analysis focuses on **identifying unintentional repetition patterns within the passage**‚Äîrepeated metaphors, descriptors, action beats, sentence structures, and emotional tells that may weaken prose through redundancy. This is NOT about exact word matches (use Word Frequency for that), but about **semantic and structural patterns** the author may not notice.

## PRIMARY EMPHASIS (80% of suggestions)

Focus on **detecting author-specific repetition patterns**:

- **Echo words**: Same unusual word appearing too close together. Not common words like "the" or "said," but distinctive words that stand out when repeated. "The *visceral* fear... the *visceral* impact" within a few paragraphs creates an unintentional echo.

- **Repeated action beats**: Characters who overuse specific physical gestures. Too many nods, sighs, shrugs, eye-rolls, hand-through-hair runs, lip-biting, jaw-clenching. These often become author tics rather than character traits.

- **Repeated metaphor families**: Multiple metaphors from the same source domain. Several knife/blade metaphors, repeated weather comparisons, consistent use of animal imagery. Pattern diversity matters.

- **Repeated descriptors**: Characters or settings always described the same way. Eyes always "piercing," smiles always "wry," movements always "fluid," rooms always "cramped." Variation creates dimension.

- **Sentence structure echoes**: Repeated syntactic patterns. Multiple "He did X. He did Y. He did Z." sequences, overuse of "As she..." openers, constant "-ing" participial phrases, repetitive compound sentence structures.

- **Repeated emotional tells**: Same physiological markers for emotions every time. Always "heart pounded" for tension, "stomach dropped" for dread, "chest tightened" for anxiety. Readers notice when the same body part responds to every emotional beat.

- **Repeated transitions/connectors**: Overuse of certain connecting phrases. Too many "suddenly," "meanwhile," "however," or "despite this." These become invisible to the author but visible to readers.

- **Repeated intensifiers**: Same emphasis words appearing frequently. "Very," "really," "absolutely," "completely," "utterly" distributed throughout. Also watch for repeated qualifiers like "seemed to," "appeared to," "almost."

## WHAT NOT TO DO

**DO NOT** suggest structural changes, plot revisions, or character development advice. Focus ONLY on repetition patterns. Examples of what to AVOID:

‚ùå "This scene needs more conflict"
‚ùå "The character should have a stronger arc"
‚ùå "The pacing feels slow here"
‚ùå "Add more dialogue to break up the prose"

**These are story-level issues, not repetition patterns.**

## SECONDARY (20% of suggestions maximum)

- **Intentional repetition recognition**: Some repetition is rhetorical. Anaphora (repeated sentence starters for emphasis), thematic echoes, or character voice consistency should be acknowledged, not flagged as problems.

- **Pattern density assessment**: A single repeated element isn't necessarily problematic. Flag when patterns become noticeable or distracting through accumulation.

Example of intentional repetition:
> "She was tired. Tired of waiting. Tired of hoping. Tired of pretending."
‚Üí This is anaphora‚Äîrhetorical repetition for emphasis. Don't flag.
