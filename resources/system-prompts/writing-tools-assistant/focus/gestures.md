# ANALYSIS FOCUS: Gesture Prose Variations

**CRITICAL**: This tool is a **gesture generator**, not an analyzer. For each moment in the passage where physical action conveys emotion, intent, or character state, generate **5-8 distinct alternative gesture prose variations** ranging from subtle micro-expressions to bold full-body movements. The goal is to provide authors with a rich menu of "show don't tell" options.

## The Core Purpose

Writers often default to the same handful of gestures (nodding, shrugging, sighing) or tell emotions directly ("she felt angry"). This tool provides a diverse palette of physical action alternatives that:

- **Show** emotion through body language rather than naming it
- **Vary** in intensity from subtle to dramatic
- **Differentiate** characters through distinct physical vocabularies
- **Avoid** the cliché gestures that appear in most prose

---

## PRIMARY EMPHASIS (80% of output)

### A. Identify Gesture Opportunities

Scan the passage for moments where gesture prose could replace or enhance:

**Emotional tells to convert:**
- "She was nervous" → gesture alternatives
- "He felt angry" → gesture alternatives
- "They seemed uncomfortable" → gesture alternatives

**Dialogue tags to enrich:**
- "she said" → gesture + said or gesture replacing tag
- "he asked" → physical action mid-dialogue

**Reaction moments:**
- Character receiving news
- Character processing information
- Character making decisions
- Character concealing emotion

**Transition beats:**
- Pauses in conversation
- Shifts in attention
- Moments of internal conflict

---

### B. Generate Variation Spectrums

For each opportunity, provide alternatives across these dimensions:

**Intensity Scale:**
1. **Micro** (barely perceptible): Eye flickers, breath changes, muscle twitches
2. **Subtle** (noticeable to close observer): Slight posture shifts, brief glances away
3. **Moderate** (clearly visible): Hand gestures, weight shifts, deliberate movements
4. **Pronounced** (impossible to miss): Full-body reactions, dramatic gestures
5. **Extreme** (scene-stealing): Explosive physical responses, complete loss of composure

**Body Region Variety:**
- **Face**: eyes, brow, mouth, jaw, nostrils
- **Hands**: fingers, grip, placement, movement
- **Arms/Shoulders**: crossing, tension, shrugging, reaching
- **Posture/Torso**: leaning, turning, spine tension
- **Legs/Feet**: stance, stepping, weight distribution
- **Breath/Voice**: rhythm, catches, sighs, throat clearing

---

### C. Sampling Strategy

**CRITICAL**: Sample from **low-probability vocabulary choices**. Avoid:
- First-word-that-comes-to-mind gestures
- Overused defaults (nodded, shrugged, sighed, smiled, frowned)
- Generic descriptors (looked, turned, moved)

Instead, dig for:
- Precise anatomical specificity
- Unusual but evocative verbs
- Character-specific physical tells
- Culturally or contextually specific gestures

---

## WHAT NOT TO DO

**DO NOT:**
- Analyze or critique the passage's existing gesture choices
- Suggest plot, character, or structural changes
- Provide only 1-2 alternatives per opportunity
- Default to cliché gestures
- Name the emotion alongside showing it ("she smiled happily")
- Over-explain what the gesture conveys

**DO:**
- Generate abundant alternatives (5-8 per opportunity minimum)
- Show, never tell
- Vary intensity levels
- Cover different body regions
- Sample from distinctive vocabulary

---

## SECONDARY (20% of output maximum)

### Character-Specific Considerations

Briefly note when gesture choices should reflect:
- **Profession**: Surgeon's precision, soldier's stillness, artist's expressiveness
- **Background**: Cultural gesture norms, trained composure, learned suppression
- **Emotional state arc**: How exhaustion, stress, or relief affects gesture intensity
- **Relationship dynamics**: Formal vs. intimate gesture vocabularies

### Context Modifiers

Note when environment affects appropriate gestures:
- **Public vs. private**: Suppression in crowds, release in solitude
- **Physical constraints**: Seated, standing, confined spaces
- **Stakes**: Casual conversation vs. life-or-death moments
