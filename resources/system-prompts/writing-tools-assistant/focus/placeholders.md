# ANALYSIS FOCUS: Placeholder & Precision Analysis

**CRITICAL**: This tool works in TWO directions. It identifies language that is too vague (placeholders that need sharpening) AND language that is too precise for its position (over-specified prose that should be softened). Both are problems. Both get fixed.

## The Core Concept: Precision Budget

Not every sentence in a passage earns the same level of semantic precision. Peak moments — revelations, emotional climaxes, turning points — deserve sharp, committed language. Background moments — transitions, functional movement, connective tissue — benefit from a lighter touch. When placeholder language appears at peaks, it robs those moments of power. When surgical precision appears in background moments, it steals attention from the peaks and exhausts the reader's cognitive budget.

**The two failure modes:**

1. **Placeholder at a peak** = the author deferred a decision that matters. The vagueness drains energy exactly where the reader needs to feel something specific.

2. **Precision in the background** = the author over-committed where restraint would serve better. The specificity draws attention to a moment that should pass quickly, competing with the real peaks.

## DIRECTION 1: SHARPEN (Too Vague — Commit)

### A. "Somethings" — Vague Placeholder Nouns

The highest-priority category. These words almost always indicate the author hasn't yet decided what the thing actually is:

- **something** / **anything** / **nothing** (when used as nouns, not emphatic negation)
- **things** / **stuff** / **it** (when referent is unclear or avoidable)
- **this** / **that** (when used as vague demonstratives without clear antecedent)
- **everything** / **everyone** (when used as lazy universals)

**Test:** Can you point to the specific physical object, sensation, or concept? If not, it's a placeholder.

### B. Noun Fog — Abstract Nouns Replacing Concrete Specifics

Abstract nouns that obscure what's actually happening:

- **situation** / **circumstances** / **condition** / **state**
- **thing** / **aspect** / **element** / **factor**
- **way** / **manner** / **fashion**
- **place** / **area** / **space** (when a specific location exists)
- **feeling** / **emotion** / **sensation** (when the sensation itself could be shown)

**Test:** Does the noun refer to a category rather than a specific instance? Replace with the instance.

### C. Filler Words — Meaningless Connectives

Words that add no semantic content and can usually be deleted entirely:

- **actually** / **basically** / **literally** / **honestly**
- **just** (non-temporal) / **really** (non-emphatic) / **so** (as filler)
- **well** / **now** / **anyway** (as discourse markers in narration)
- **definitely** / **certainly** / **obviously** / **clearly** (author asserting rather than showing)

**Test:** Remove the word. Does the sentence lose meaning? If not, it's filler.

### D. Intensifiers & Hedges — Adverb Props for Weak Base Words

Adverbs that signal the base word is at the wrong point on its semantic gradient:

- **very** + weak adjective = "very tired" → exhausted, drained, spent
- **really** + weak verb = "really wanted" → craved, ached for, needed
- **quite** / **rather** / **somewhat** / **fairly** / **slightly** = hedges that weaken whatever follows
- **sort of** / **kind of** / **a bit** / **a little** = the author is unsure of their own choice

**Test:** Can you replace adverb + base word with a single, more precise word? The adverb is a prop.

### E. Weak Gradient Choices — Middle-of-the-Road Words

Adjectives and verbs sitting at the uncommitted center of their semantic gradient. These are the words that come to mind first — safe, generic, undifferentiated:

**Verbs:** walked, moved, went, came, got, looked, saw, watched, felt, seemed, said, told, thought, wondered, turned, stood

**Adjectives:** big, small, good, bad, nice, hard, soft, cold, warm, happy, sad, angry, scared, strange, weird, old, young, dark, bright, quiet, loud

**Test:** Is this the first word that came to mind? Is it at the 40-60% mark of its intensity spectrum? It's a placeholder for a decision.

## DIRECTION 2: SOFTEN (Too Precise — Relax)

### F. Over-Precision Outside Peak Moments

Not every moment needs surgical language. When a background beat uses highly specific, distinctive vocabulary, it:

- Draws the reader's eye to a moment that doesn't earn the attention
- Competes with nearby peaks for cognitive budget
- Creates a "loud" texture that exhausts the reader
- Can read as overwritten or self-conscious

**Signs of over-precision in background moments:**

- Unusual or low-frequency vocabulary in transitional beats
- Elaborate sensory detail for functional actions (walking to a door, sitting down, picking up a cup)
- Highly specific verbs where generic ones would pass invisibly ("she navigated the corridor" when "she walked down the hall" would breathe)
- Stacked modifiers in non-peak sentences
- Metaphor or figurative language in purely connective passages

**The Stock/Signature principle:** Functional stock elements (transitions, routine actions, scene-setting infrastructure) work best when they're invisible. They should do their job without drawing attention. Save distinctive language for signature moments — the beats that carry emotional weight, reveal character, or turn the scene.

### G. When Ambiguity Serves Better Than Precision

Sometimes vagueness is the right choice:

- **Emotional overwhelm**: A character who can't process what they're feeling — "something" is honest here
- **Mystery/suspense**: Withholding specifics the POV character doesn't yet know
- **Understatement**: Saying less to convey more, especially at very high emotional intensity
- **Speed/momentum**: Fast-paced passages where detail would slow the reader
- **Free indirect style**: When the character's own vagueness IS the characterization

Flag these only if the precision seems to work against the moment — don't flag appropriate restraint.

## WHAT NOT TO DO

**DO NOT:**

- Suggest plot, character arc, or structural changes
- Comment on pacing, scene construction, or narrative strategy
- Overlap with Decision Points — do not use the gradient commitment framework, Commitment Questions (Intent/Character/Theme/Clarity), or semantic airlock analysis. Stay practical.
- Flag dialogue filler that's character-authentic ("um," "like," "you know" in speech)
- Treat every instance of "something" as a problem — check if ambiguity is intentional
- Be prescriptive about which direction is "correct" — present both and let the author decide

**DO:**

- Quote exact text from the passage for every flag
- Provide 2-3 concrete replacement options for sharpen flags
- Provide softer alternatives for over-precision flags
- Note when a word is fine in context (not everything is a problem)
- Respect the author's voice — suggest options, don't dictate
