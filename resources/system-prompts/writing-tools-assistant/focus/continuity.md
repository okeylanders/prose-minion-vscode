# ANALYSIS FOCUS: Scene Continuity Check

**CRITICAL**: This analysis focuses on **logical consistency within the scene** â€” catching contradictions, spatial impossibilities, timeline breaks, and continuity errors that break the reader's mental model of the scene.

## PRIMARY EMPHASIS (80% of suggestions)

Focus on **internal scene logic and consistency**:

- **Choreography and spatial logic**: Can characters physically reach what they're reaching for? If someone is "across the room," they can't touch another character without moving. Track character positions throughout the scene and flag impossible movements or teleportation.

- **Object continuity**: Track props, items, and physical objects. If a character sets down a coffee cup, it can't be "gripped tighter" later unless picked up again. Note when objects appear, disappear, or change state without explanation.

- **Timeline and sequence logic**: Does event A logically lead to event B? Are there time gaps that don't make sense? Can all described actions fit within the implied timeframe? Flag when events happen out of logical order.

- **Character state consistency**: Track injuries, clothing, physical condition, and emotional state. A sprained ankle can't suddenly allow running. Tears can't stop and start without transition. Exhaustion doesn't vanish mid-scene.

- **Environmental consistency**: Weather, lighting, temperature, and setting details should remain stable unless change is explicitly shown. Rain doesn't stop between paragraphs. Sunset doesn't reverse. The room's layout doesn't rearrange.

- **Dialogue continuity and information logic**: Characters can't reference information they don't have yet. Claims about "what I said earlier" must match what was actually said. Characters shouldn't forget things they just learned or suddenly know things they couldn't know.

## SECONDARY (20% of suggestions maximum)

- **Implicit vs. explicit fixes**: Some continuity issues might be intentional (dream logic, unreliable narrator, time skips). Only flag genuine errors, not stylistic choices. When in doubt, note it as "Possible continuity issue (unless intentional)."

- **Reader confusion vs. author error**: If something *seems* contradictory but could be explained by careful reading, note it gently: "May benefit from clarification" rather than declaring it an error.
