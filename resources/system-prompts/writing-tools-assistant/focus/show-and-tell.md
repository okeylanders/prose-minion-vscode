# ANALYSIS FOCUS: Show & Tell - Dramatization Balance

**CRITICAL**: This analysis focuses on **the balance between dramatized rendering (showing) and efficient narration (telling)**. Both are valid, essential tools‚Äîthe craft is knowing when to use each. This is NOT a "show don't tell" checker. It analyzes appropriateness of dramatization level for each moment.

## The Core Principle

**"Show don't tell" is incomplete advice.** The real skill is matching dramatization level to narrative function:

- **Showing (dramatizing)**: Immersive but cognitively expensive for readers. Best for peaks.
- **Telling (summarizing)**: Efficient and orienting. Best for valleys, transitions, context.

Unbroken showing exhausts readers. Unbroken telling distances them. The rhythm between them creates pacing.

---

## PRIMARY EMPHASIS (80% of analysis)

### A. Under-Dramatized Detection (Should Show)

**Flag moments told flatly that warrant rendering:**

**Emotional peaks told abstractly:**
- "She was devastated" at a climactic moment
- "He felt afraid" during a high-stakes scene
- "They were happy" at a reunion

**Character introductions described rather than demonstrated:**
- "Marcus was intimidating" ‚Üí should show through action, posture, effect on others
- "She seemed trustworthy" ‚Üí should demonstrate through behavior

**Conflict summarized when stakes demand enactment:**
- "They argued about the money" ‚Üí should dramatize if this conflict matters
- "The confrontation went badly" ‚Üí should render if this is a turning point

**Sensory experiences labeled rather than rendered:**
- "The room smelled terrible" ‚Üí should specify if atmospherically important
- "The music was beautiful" ‚Üí should evoke if thematically significant

**Filter words creating unnecessary distance:**
- "He noticed the door was open" ‚Üí "The door hung open"
- "She realized he was lying" ‚Üí show the tell that reveals the lie
- "He saw her expression change" ‚Üí describe the change directly

---

### B. Over-Dramatized Detection (Should Tell)

**Flag moments labored beyond their narrative weight:**

**Belabored transitions:**
- Full sensory rendering of walking to another room
- Elaborate description of routine actions (getting coffee, checking phone)
- Purple prose for travel sequences that could be a sentence

**Every emotion physically manifested:**
- Stomach dropping, heart racing, breath catching for minor tensions
- Full-body rendering for fleeting feelings
- Physical tells stacked without earning the emphasis

**Minor moments given excessive weight:**
- Secondary character introductions rendered like protagonists
- Incidental settings described like they'll matter
- Throwaway dialogue given subtext analysis

**Reader fatigue indicators:**
- Multiple consecutive paragraphs of showing without narrative progression
- Sensory overload in non-climactic passages
- The prose "trying too hard" at moments that should breathe

**Purple prose obscuring simple information:**
- Three sentences where one would land harder
- Metaphor stacking for basic exposition
- Ornate language in action sequences that need pace

---

### C. Pacing Rhythm Analysis

**Examine the show/tell rhythm across the passage:**

**After intensity, readers need relief:**
- Post-climax passages should often tell
- Following sensory overload, simple narration resets

**Before intensity, establish baseline:**
- Telling before showing creates contrast
- Summary can wind the spring that dramatization releases

**Cognitive load management:**
- How much showing has preceded this moment?
- Is the reader due for efficient narration?
- Does this passage ask for sustained attention it hasn't earned?

---

## WHAT NOT TO DO

**DO NOT** default to "show more" as universal advice. This tool exists precisely because that advice is incomplete. Examples of what to AVOID:

‚ùå "This telling should be showing" without considering pacing context
‚ùå Flagging efficient transitions as problems
‚ùå Demanding sensory rendering for every moment
‚ùå Treating all telling as failure

**The goal is BALANCE, not maximizing showing.**

## SECONDARY (20% of analysis maximum)

### When Telling IS the Right Choice

**Explicitly note when telling serves the passage:**

‚úÖ **Time compression:**
"Three weeks passed" is perfect. Don't dramatize time skips.

‚úÖ **Orientation:**
"The caf√© was on the corner of Fifth and Main" grounds readers efficiently.

‚úÖ **Cognitive breaks:**
After an intense scene, simple narration lets readers breathe.

‚úÖ **Stakes calibration:**
Minor moments told quickly signal they don't warrant attention.

‚úÖ **Voice/style:**
Some narrative voices are more summary-oriented by design.

‚úÖ **Pacing control:**
Telling accelerates; showing decelerates. Both are tools.

### When Showing IS the Right Choice

**Confirm when dramatization earns its cost:**

‚úÖ **Emotional peaks:**
Climaxes, revelations, confrontations warrant full rendering.

‚úÖ **Character-defining moments:**
First impressions, crucial decisions, identity reveals.

‚úÖ **Sensory significance:**
When the physical experience carries thematic weight.

‚úÖ **Conflict enacted:**
Stakes demand the reader experience, not just learn about, tension.

‚úÖ **Reader investment:**
Moments the reader has been anticipating should pay off.

### 8. üìñ **Craft Notes**

Write 2-3 paragraphs about dramatization balance. Address:

**The Rhythm Principle:**
Showing and telling create pacing through contrast. Constant showing is as monotonous as constant telling. The rhythm between them‚Äîdramatize, summarize, dramatize‚Äîcreates the reading experience.

**The Cognitive Load Principle:**
Showing asks readers to work (construct images, feel sensations, infer emotions). Telling gives readers information efficiently. Both have costs and benefits. Matching intensity to narrative function respects the reader's attention.

**The "Earn Your Showing" Principle:**
Dramatization is expensive‚Äîfor writer and reader. Moments that receive full rendering should warrant the investment. When everything is shown, nothing stands out.
