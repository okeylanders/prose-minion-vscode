# Moment Descriptors

## Categories

This document collects words and short phrases you can use to name and shape "moments" in prose. Moments are units of attention — temporal, sensory, emotional, spatial, or artful — that authors can spotlight to control pacing, emphasis, and atmosphere. Below the classic categories are additions drawn from photography, film, music, theater, and visual art to give you extra metaphors and precise tools for scene-crafting.

## Cross-Modal Examples (how these terms map to prose)

- "A close-up on Kayla's hand, the world blurred into bokeh." (photography → detail)
- "The hallway played like an interlude — one long take of lockers and elbows." (film → sustained attention)
- "Their conversation hit a chorus: the same argument, returning in a little refrains." (music → repeating motif)
- "He stepped into the doorway; it was an establishing shot that framed him as something new." (film/photography → characterization)
- "A soft- focus moment softened the edges of grief into something tender and manageable." (photography → emotional haze)
- "The house fell into a blackout between them — a silence so complete it felt stage-lit and deliberate." (theater → controlled pause)
- "A fermata hung on his breath; no one moved until it broke." (music → held breath)
- "The memory came as a vignette: one small scene peeled free from the rest of the day." (visual art → isolated detail)
- "Her resolve was a dolly-in: everything tightened toward the face until the decision filled the frame." (film → intensifying focus)
- "Their laughter swelled like a crescendo and then let go." (music → emotional arc)

---

## Usage Notes & Guidance

- Choose a disciplinary metaphor to set the perceptual lens of the scene. "Frame" or "close-up" tells readers to notice detail; "establishing shot" sets spatial context.
- Combine categories for texture: a "kairos-frame" (sacred moment + photographic frame) directs attention toward spiritual significance within a focused image.
- Use music terms to indicate pacing and repetition: "refrain" suggests recurrence; "crescendo" signals building emotional intensity.
- Micro-moment descriptors help you control sentence rhythm. Short verbs (cut, snap, hush) increase pace; longer phrases (a long take, suspended second) slow it.
- Avoid mixing metaphors carelessly. Pick the lens that best serves the scene's emotional and pacing goals. Cross-modal blends can be powerful when intentional ("a freeze-frame of mercy").
- When in doubt, ask: What should the reader attend to in this second? Form (image, sound, beat) → choose the matching descriptor.

## Examples (phrases to drop directly into prose)

- "A golden-hour frame held them, soft and decisive."
- "The argument came in a sudden cut; silence filled the black space between the lines."
- "He carried the refrain of her laugh like an answer he couldn't yet read."
- "For a single aperture moment, everything aligned: light, breath, the tilt of her head."
- "They shared a tableau of resignation, frozen until someone blinked."
- "A fermata hovered on his apology and then released it like a bell."
- "She felt like an underexposed photograph, all shadow and suggestion until someone turned the light."
