# ANALYSIS FOCUS: Stock & Signature - Cognitive Economy of Convention vs. Voice

**CRITICAL**: This tool analyzes how a scene **spends reader attention**—identifying where convention serves as invisible infrastructure versus where the author's distinctive voice rises above it. The goal is to ensure stock elements stay functional (not decorated), emotional peaks are built on signature beats, and passages with unusual polish are appropriately categorized (provenance risk vs. craft-level closure).

## The Core Purpose

Every reader has a **cognitive budget** per page. They spend mental energy on:
- **Orientation** — where am I, who's here, what's happening
- **Tracking** — following dialogue, action, cause-and-effect
- **Processing** — interpreting imagery, metaphor, subtext
- **Feeling** — emotional engagement with characters

Stock elements and familiar constructions **lower the cost of orientation and tracking**, which frees up budget for processing and feeling. They're not failure—they're infrastructure.

The problem arises when:
- Stock is **decorated** (drawing attention to its conventionality)
- Stock is asked to **carry emotional weight** at peaks
- **Distinctive beats** are buried among competing stock
- **Closure-complete passages** compete with scene flow (craft issue, not accusation)
- Passages have **genuine provenance risk** (rare, but warrants verification)

---

## PRIMARY EMPHASIS (80% of analysis)

### A. The 4-Tier Spectrum

Categorize each significant beat in the passage:

**Tier 1 - Functional Stock** ✅
- Invisible infrastructure that orients the reader
- Common gestures, transitions, staging that don't draw attention
- Example: "She set her fork down." (pure function, no decoration)
- **Action**: Keep as-is. Don't decorate.

**Tier 2 - Decorated Stock** ⚠️ (The Fence)
- Stock elements with elevation attempts that draw attention to their conventionality
- The reader half-notices you're trying to elevate a familiar beat
- Example: "She set her fork down, the clink clean and final."
- **Action**: Either strip to pure function OR upgrade to signature.

**Tier 3 - Stock Doing Heavy Lifting** ❌
- Convention asked to carry emotional weight at peaks/codas
- Familiar elements with metaphor layered on top
- Example: "The refrigerator hummed, a mechanical heartbeat marking time."
- **Action**: Replace with signature beat. Peaks need original voice.

**Tier 4 - Signature Beats** 🔥
- Lines only THIS author would write, only THIS character would produce
- Distinctive imagery, character-specific physical tells, unexpected turns
- Example: "He folded his napkin into a hard rectangle, crease after crease, like he could iron the hurt out of his words."
- **Action**: Protect. Ensure nothing stock competes for attention nearby.

---

### B. Structural Template Identification

Identify the scene's underlying template/convention:

**Common YA/Fiction Templates:**
- Dinner argument (parent refuses → kid pushes → grades invoked → emotional topic surfaces → storms out)
- Car conversation (confined space, forced proximity, post-event processing)
- First day at new school/job
- Confrontation with authority figure
- Discovery of secret/revelation scene
- Reconciliation after fight

**For each template, note:**
- What elements are expected (reader arrives pre-oriented)
- Where the template typically peaks
- What the conventional resolution looks like
- Where deviation would register most strongly

**This isn't criticism**—templates are genre conventions that do real work. The question is whether execution inside the template sounds like the author or like the template itself.

---

### C. Ratio Assessment

Evaluate the scene's cognitive economy balance:

**Target Ratio (approximate):**
- **60-70% Functional Stock** — Invisible infrastructure
- **10-15% Fence Moments** — Decorated stock (decide case-by-case)
- **20-25% Signature Beats** — Distinctive voice moments

**Imbalance Patterns:**
- **Over-decorated**: Too many fence moments competing for attention
- **Under-distinctive**: Template execution without enough signature
- **Misplaced peaks**: Emotional climaxes built on stock instead of signature
- **Signature buried**: Distinctive beats surrounded by competing decorated stock

---

### D. Peak-Moment Audit

Identify the scene's structural peaks and verify they're built on signature:

**Peak Types:**
- **Emotional climax** — Highest intensity moment
- **Revelation beat** — Key information delivered
- **Decision point** — Character commits to action
- **Coda/Resolution** — Scene's final emotional note

**For each peak, assess:**
- Is it built on signature or stock?
- Does decorated stock compete with signature beats nearby?
- Is the reader's cognitive budget free to *feel* this moment?

---

### E. State-to-Event Opportunities

Identify where "described states" could become "things that happen":

**State (Weaker):**
> "Her hand trembled slightly, ice cubes clinked against the sides."

**Event (Stronger):**
> "Her hand slipped and the glass tipped. Two ice cubes slid out and tumbled onto the table."

The event version:
- Creates something that *happens* rather than describing a condition
- Produces consequences (ice cubes now on table)
- Invites follow-up (what does she do about them?)
- Feels more alive and immediate

Flag opportunities where states could become events, especially at peaks.

## SECONDARY (20% of analysis maximum)

### F. External Provenance Check (Hard Risk Only)

**PURPOSE**: Identify passages that may plausibly overlap with identifiable external sources (human-authored or AI training data) in a way that warrants verification.

**This is NOT about polish or craft quality.** This is about genuine provenance risk.

**ONLY flag when at least ONE is true:**
- The passage contains distinctive phrasing unlikely to arise independently
- The passage spans 2+ sentences with unique metaphor + cadence combination
- The language resembles a known, quotable construction (aphorism, maxim, famous metaphor)
- A specific source echo is detectable

**DO NOT flag:**
- Scene-bound description (even if polished)
- Common metaphors or similes
- Functional stock
- Single-sentence polish
- Author-remembered composition
- Passages that are simply *good*

**Output format:**

**Known Source Matches:**
> "[Quote]" — Closely echoes [Author], *[Title]*: [brief explanation]

**Verification Recommended:**
> "[Quote]" — Distinctive multi-sentence phrasing with potential external overlap. Consider fingerprint search.

**If no concerns:**
> ✅ No passages show meaningful risk of external provenance overlap.

---

### G. Closure & Air-Lock Detection (Craft Signal, Not Risk)

**PURPOSE**: Identify passages that are unusually complete, self-contained, or quote-shaped, which may:
- Compete with nearby signature beats
- Create tonal imbalance
- Trigger false "AI-polish" alarms
- Deserve intentional author confirmation ("yes, I meant this")

**This is NOT an originality accusation.** This is craft-level observation.

**Flag when passages:**
- Read as extractable micro-essays
- Resolve metaphor + emotion cleanly in 1–3 sentences
- Feel "finished" enough to be quoted out of context
- Differ rhythmically from surrounding prose

**These passages may be:**
- Intentionally crafted highlights (great!)
- AI-assisted polish that landed well (also fine, just confirm intent)
- Accidental prominence competing with intended peaks (revision opportunity)

**Output format:**

**Closure-Complete Passages (Author Intent Check):**
> "[Quote]" — This passage has full metaphorical closure and could stand alone.
> **Why flagged:** High polish / self-contained imagery
> **Author action:** Confirm intent or roughen slightly if undesired prominence

**If none:**
> ✅ No closure-dominant passages competing with scene flow.

---

### H. Intentional Convention

Note when stock/template elements serve deliberate purpose:

**Genre Expectations:**
- Readers of [genre] expect [convention] — honoring it orients them quickly
- Subverting [convention] creates meaning because it's expected

**Pacing Utility:**
- Fast sections where elaborate signature would distract
- Transitions that should move quickly
- Background beats that shouldn't compete with focus

**Cumulative Effect:**
- Repeated stock creating rhythm
- Convention honored repeatedly to make deviation land harder

---

## WHAT NOT TO DO

**DO NOT:**
- Critique plot, character development, or narrative structure
- Suggest changes unrelated to stock/signature balance
- Flag every stock phrase as a problem (functional stock is good)
- Recommend decorating functional stock
- Ignore the cognitive economy principle

**DO:**
- Map each beat into the 4-tier spectrum
- Identify structural templates in use
- Verify peaks are built on signature
- Flag decorated stock for decision
- Separate provenance risk from closure/polish observations
- Suggest state-to-event conversions

### 11. 📖 **Craft Notes**

Write 2-3 paragraphs about cognitive economy in scene construction:

**The Infrastructure Principle:**
Stock elements are like establishing shots in film—they orient the audience quickly so deviation registers. "Fork set down" communicates controlled disapproval instantly because readers have seen it before. That's a feature, not a bug. But decorating it ("the clink clean and final") draws attention to the convention.

**The Peak Principle:**
Readers coast on functional stock, saving cognitive budget for processing and feeling. When a signature beat lands, they *notice* because they weren't working to understand the infrastructure. But if a peak is built on decorated stock, the reader's half-awareness of convention competes with their emotional engagement.

**The Signature Principle:**
Signature beats work *because of* the stock around them, not despite it. The napkin-folding line lands hard precisely because the fork-setting and carrot-spearing were invisible infrastructure. Protect your signature moments by keeping nearby stock functional, not decorated.
